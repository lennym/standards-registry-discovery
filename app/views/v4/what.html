Using standards to build interoperable services

All digital products and services should use approved standardised formats - standards - for collecting, structuring and exchanging health and social care data in England.

Using these standards consistently allows IT systems and products to communicate across care settings, and for permitted staff to be able to directly access or share certain information with those settings. This is known as interoperability.

There are two types of standard that form the main building blocks of interoperable digital products and services.

Content standards

Content standards ensure information is collected and formatted consistently. They ensure information:

  • is grouped under the same headings - ‘Person’s details’ instead of ‘Patient’s details’, for example
  • uses the correct clinical codes - for example, recording someone’s medication using its dedicated code rather than just writing the name which can lead to errors

Technical standards

Technical or messaging standards ensure information is structured in code in a consistent way that can be easily shared with other systems. These are often built into APIs to make them easier to implement.

Why use standards

The use of standards and the development of interoperable services is important for the safety of people receiving care. This is because:

  • it reduces the risk of errors - for example, if a staff member writes down the name of someone’s medication without including the correct code it could be misread by a colleague
  • it gives permitted health and social care professionals instant access to someone’s records - in an emergency situation this could save someone’s life

This is why some standards are mandatory for use in all circumstances where they apply.

There are also major benefits for people who give and receive care.

Benefits to people who receive care

Benefits include:

  • reducing the burden on people needing to share details about their health or treatment every time they receive some form of care. This can be hard for everyone, but especially for people with conditions that affect their memory.
  • allowing people to be able to see their own health record, so they have more visibility and ownership of their data
  • giving people more effective care by ensuring healthcare professionals make informed decisions based on their full history

Benefits to health and social care professionals

Benefits include:

  • being able to call up someone’s health record or a snapshot of their record instantly without having to ask anyone to share it
  • being able to see richer details about the health and wellbeing of the person they’re caring for to better tailor treatment or services
  • less time filling in paperwork

The recent pandemic - where we had to quickly move patients to wherever there was space to treat them - showed us how important it is to join up IT systems to make care provision easier.

You can see some of the real benefits people have experienced thanks to interoperable technology in case studies for Manchester and Swindon.

How to use standards

For health and social care systems to benefit from exchanging information you need to:

  • use standards correctly
  • follow rules on data security
  • agree rules on information governance
  • ensure staff use new ways of working

Using standards correctly

To ensure data is correctly exchanged, you must use content and technical standards in every system that wants to share or receive information.

If the correct standards aren’t used, the data will not be exchanged or it will not render correctly at the receiving end. For example, to correctly exchange information when discharging someone from a mental health to a GP service you need:

  • the content standard for mental health discharge to ensure the systems sending and receiving the information format it in the same way
  • the technical standard for mental health discharge to ensure the data is structured in a way that can be exchanged

However simply exchanging the information isn’t enough. There are also other important issues to consider.

Agreeing rules on information governance

Before exchanging information, care settings need to agree rules around information governance. This includes deciding who will be able to see what data and agreeing a process for recording patients’ and service users’ consent.

Changes to ways of working

For staff to be able to see information across organisational boundaries, data needs to be recorded using digital systems. If staff continue to record and share information using paper, email or fax the benefits of interoperability won’t be felt by them or the people they’re caring for.

Contributing to standards development

Standards don’t exist for everything services are trying to do in health and social care. In some cases content standards might exist but corresponding technical standards might not.

What’s important is that where standards and APIs do exist and apply, they are used. If they don’t exist there’s an opportunity to contribute a standard or API to the community to support standardisation in that particular area.
